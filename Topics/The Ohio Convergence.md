# The Ohio Convergence: Gathering of the Clans

Before the Thorpe family line firmly established itself in the rich soil of Iowa, the various branches of the family first converged in the developing state of Ohio. During the mid-19th century, counties like **Jefferson, Meigs, and Fairfield** became the crucible where the Ault, Tallman, and Lemmon families gathered.

## The Ault Household in Jefferson County

The story of the convergence begins in **Jefferson County** in the 1850s. Here, we find the household of **[[People/Frederick Ault|Frederick Ault]]**, a carpenter from Pennsylvania. His daughter, **[[People/Elizabeth Plomey Ault|Elizabeth Plomey Ault]]**, was growing up in a world where the frontier was rapidly moving westward. Nearby, the **[[People/Andrew Ault|Andrew Ault]]** household represented the older generation, anchoring the family's presence in the Ohio River valley.

## The Tallman-Boone Connection

Further west in **Fairfield County**, the Tallman family was already legendary. **[[People/Benjamin Tallman 1745-1820|Benjamin Tallman]]** and his wife **[[People/Dinah Boone|Dinah Boone]]** (the first cousin of the frontiersman Daniel Boone) had settled there around 1810. They were part of a massive migration from Virginia and Pennsylvania, bringing with them a legacy of Revolutionary War service and a pioneering spirit.

By the 1840s, the next generation—including **[[People/Benjamin B Tallman|Benjamin B. Tallman]]** and his wife **[[People/Romancy Miller|Romancy Miller]]**—were managing large farming operations. It was here that the family consolidated its resources, preparing for the final push into the Iowa prairie.

## The Gathering in Sandusky

To the north, in **Sandusky County**, the Lemmon and MacIntyre families were carving out their own history. **[[People/Uriah Blake Lemmon|Uriah Blake Lemmon]]** established a home there that would eventually produce the links to the Thorpe line. This county, with its proximity to Lake Erie, offered a different economic landscape than the southern Ohio hills, but the shared goal was the same: land ownership and family stability.

## The Push to Iowa

The "Ohio Convergence" was not just a period of settlement; it was a gathering of strength. By the late 1840s and early 1850s, the "Iowa Fever" had taken hold. One by one, these families packed their wagons and moved westward again. The Tallmans and Aults would reunite in **Linn County, Iowa**, while the Lemmon line would eventually merge with the Thorpes, completing the multi-generational journey that started in the valleys of Ohio.
