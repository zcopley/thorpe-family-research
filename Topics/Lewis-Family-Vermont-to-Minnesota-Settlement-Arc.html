Lewis Family Vermont to Minnesota Settlement Arc

Overview

The Lewis family migration from Vermont (1782–1850, 68 years) through Wisconsin (1850–1880, 30 years) to Minnesota (1880–1923, 43 years) represents a 140-year multi-generational settlement journey spanning three states and three distinct agricultural regions. The narrative documents how a Vermont farming family adapted to changing frontier conditions, established land ownership, developed multi-generational farming operations, and ultimately consolidated in the Midwest agricultural heartland. This arc illustrates successful frontier settlement patterns, occupational continuity across regions, and family economic advancement through agricultural land acquisition.

The Journey: A Three-Generation Story

Generation 1: Wynat Lewis — Vermont Patriarch (1782–1850s)

Vermont Origins (1782–1850s, 68+ years)

Biographical Context:

  • Birth: ~1782 (estimates range 1768–1790; recorded as “Wynat” or “W.W. Lewis”)
  • Birthplace: Vermont (later censuses recorded Massachusetts, suggesting possible intermediate settlement)
  • Occupation: Farmer (consistent across documented residences)
  • Family status: Married to Susan Lewis (~1796 birth); 50-year marriage (married ~1800)
  • Children: Eliza (b. ~1813), Alonzo (b. ~1817), Oliver Warren (1823–1892), Martha/Marth (b. ~1831), plus extended household members

Vermont Residence (1782–1850):

  • 68-year Vermont life span; farming primary occupation throughout
  • Vermont-born children (Eliza, Alonzo, Oliver Warren, Martha) born between 1813–1831
  • Latest documented Vermont child: Martha (b. ~1831); suggests continued Vermont residence through 1830s
  • No documented census before 1850; intermediate settlement locations unclear

Economic Status:

  • Farming occupation suggests modest property ownership or leasehold farming
  • Multi-child household indicates family labor available
  • Ability to migrate at advanced age (1850, age 68) suggests modest capital for relocation

Generation 1 Continued: Wisconsin Settlement (1850–c.1860s)

Racine County, Wisconsin Entry (1850)

Journey Details:

  • Age at arrival: Wynat Lewis age 68 in 1850; significant relocation at advanced age
  • Location: Racine County, Town of Burlington, Wisconsin
  • Household composition: 8 members (Wynat, Susan, Eliza age 37, Alonzo age 33, Oliver age 25, Martha age 19, Erastus Darling age 15, Sarah Darling age 12)
  • Occupational status: Wynat and Alonzo both listed as farmers; farming continuation evident
  • Settlement timing: 1850 census represents first documented Wisconsin residence; arrival date unknown (could be 1840s or earlier)

Economic Transition:

  • Moved from Vermont subsistence farming to Wisconsin frontier agricultural settlement
  • Lake Michigan/Erie Canal region provided transportation and market access
  • Burlington Township: established agricultural settlement area; suggests family chose developed community

Occupational Continuity:

  • Farming remained primary family occupation
  • Multiple adult sons (Alonzo, Oliver) engaged in farming; suggests family farming operation
  • Extended household (8 members) enabled shared agricultural labor

Fond du Lac County, Wisconsin Continuation (1860)

Geographic Shift Details:

  • Movement: Racine County rural (1850) → Fond du Lac County urban/village (1860)
  • Location: Fond du Lac County, Ripon 1st Ward (urban setting)
  • Household composition: 6 members (W.W. Lewis age 79, Susan age 58, Oliver age 35, Marshall age 25, Elizabeth age 24, Martha E. age 7/12)
  • Occupational status: W.W. Lewis and Oliver both farmers; farming continued despite urban location
  • Family expansion: Martha E. Lewis born Wisconsin 1859; infant documented in 1860 census

Life Stage Transition:

  • Wynat age progression: 68 (1850 Racine) → 79 (1860 Fond du Lac) = 10-year span documented
  • Occupational sustainability: Patriarch continued farming at age 79; extreme advanced-age farming
  • Household changes: Smaller household (6 vs. 8 members 1850); extended family members (Darling) dispersed; new household members (Marshall, Elizabeth) documented
  • Property status: $50 recorded property value; minimal property documentation (may reflect leasehold vs. freehold)

Migration Significance:

  • 10-year Wisconsin residence (1850–1860); intermediate settlement point between Vermont and Minnesota
  • Rural-to-urban transition (Burlington → Ripon Ward) suggests economic adaptation
  • Family consolidation: Martha E. Lewis born 1859 shows family establishment; transition from migration to settlement

Patriarch’s Death:

  • Wynat Lewis death likely late 1860s (c.1867–1870 estimated, documented as age ~80+)
  • Vermont-to-Wisconsin-to-Minnesota family transferred to next generation

Generation 2: Oliver Warren Lewis — Wisconsin-to-Minnesota Settler (1823–1892)

Wisconsin Farming (1850–1880, age 25–57)

Early Wisconsin Settlement (Racine County 1850):

  • Age/status: Oliver Warren Lewis age 25 in 1850; young adult farmer in family operation
  • Occupation: Listed as farmer; engaged in family farming with father Wynat
  • Family role: Son transitioning toward independent farming
  • Life stage: Young adult establishing farming skills; prepared for independent operation

Wisconsin Continuation (Fond du Lac County 1860):

  • Age/status: Oliver Warren Lewis age 35 in 1860; prime working-age farmer
  • Occupation: Farmer; independent adult role evident
  • Household: Wife Susan (not documented 1850; married 1850–1860) and infant daughter Martha E. Lewis (1859 Wisconsin-born)
  • Implication: Oliver established independent household and family by 1860; family consolidation evident

Wisconsin Economic Establishment:

  • 10-year Wisconsin residence (1850–1860) establishing farming operations
  • Marriage and child birth (Martha E. 1859) indicate family establishment
  • Farming occupation maintained; economic independence achieved
  • Property status: modest (inferred from $50 property value family documentation)

Transition to Minnesota (1880)

Mower County, Minnesota Settlement (1880):

  • Age/status: Oliver Warren Lewis age 57 in 1880; mature farmer, 30 years after Wisconsin settlement began
  • Location: Mower County, Grand Meadow Township, Minnesota
  • Household composition: 4 members (Oliver age 57, Elizabeth age 44 wife, George age 16 son working on farm, William age 9 son at school)
  • Occupational status: Farmer; continued farming across 30-year span (Wisconsin 1850–1880)
  • Geographic shift: Wisconsin (1850–1880, 30 years) → Minnesota (1880 documented, likely 1880–1892)

Settlement Pattern:

  • Vermont-born (1823) → Wisconsin farming (1850–1880, age 25–57) → Minnesota farming (1880–1892, age 57–69)
  • Three-state, three-region agricultural progression across 70-year lifespan
  • Consistent farming occupation across all regions and life stages
  • Economic advancement: independent farmer status achieved in Wisconsin, maintained in Minnesota

Later Life and Death:

  • Oliver Warren Lewis death 1892, age 69 (died while farming or in early retirement)
  • 70-year farming career from age 25 (Wisconsin 1850) to age 69 (Minnesota 1892)
  • Successful frontier settler; established family economic independence through farming

Generation 3: Martha Eliza Lewis → Martha Eliza Prior — Granddaughter Settlement Extension (1859–1923)

Wisconsin Birth and Childhood (1859–1870)

Birth Context:

  • Birth: December 15, 1859, Wisconsin (Fond du Lac County or nearby)
  • Parentage: Daughter of Oliver Warren Lewis (farmer) and Elizabeth Lewis
  • Birthplace: Wisconsin; represents first generation born outside Vermont
  • Significance: Infant Martha E. documented in 1860 Fond du Lac County census; Wisconsin-born indicates family establishment

Childhood in Wisconsin:

  • Years 1859–1880: documented childhood through age 20 in Wisconsin/Minnesota region
  • Wisconsin farming household context: grandmother Wynat Lewis, grandfather Oliver Warren Lewis
  • Multi-generational household; inherited farming family values and occupational traditions

Minnesota Settlement and Marriage (1880–1900)

Marriage and Family Formation:

  • Marriage: c.1880 (inferred from 1900 census documentation)
  • Spouse: Arthur Edwin Prior (1853–?, farmer, Michigan-born)
  • Union significance: Lewis family married into Prior family; unified two Midwest farming families

Mower County, Minnesota Household (1900):

  • Age/status: Martha Eliza Lewis age 40 in 1900; wife of farmer Arthur Prior
  • Household composition: 7 members (Arthur Prior farmer age 46, Martha age 40, Oliver W. Prior age 20 farm laborer son, George W. Prior age 16 farm laborer son, Lewis H. Prior age 11 son, Viva P. Prior age 4 daughter, Lora P. Prior age 2 daughter)
  • Occupational role: Documented as wife (no occupational designation, though “Keeping House” implied); household management role
  • Economic status: Multi-child farm family; extended household suggesting prosperous farming operation

Intermediate Settlement (South Dakota, 1910):

  • Geographic shift: Minnesota → Brown County, South Dakota (Hanson Township) 1910
  • Family status: Continued with Arthur Prior; 4 children documented (including Lora, Arthur Jr.)
  • Duration: Likely temporary (economic hardship or opportunity-driven migration)
  • Return to Minnesota: Documented 1920 census Pine County, Minnesota (Pokegama Township); returned to Minnesota

Minnesota Consolidation (1920–1923):

  • Age/status: Martha Eliza Prior age 60 in 1920; mature farm household manager
  • Location: Pine County, Minnesota (Pokegama Township)
  • Household: Arthur E. Prior (age 66 farmer), Martha E. Prior (age 60, house keeping), Arthur E. Prior Jr. (age 16)
  • Economic status: Established farming household; multi-decade Minnesota residence
  • Death: December 8, 1923, age 63; burial Brook Park Cemetery, Minnesota

Occupational Continuity

  • Farming across all regions: Vermont (Wynat) → Wisconsin (Wynat + Oliver) → Minnesota (Oliver + Martha Prior household)
  • Three-generation farming: Wynat (patriarch) → Oliver (son) → Martha Eliza (granddaughter as farmer’s wife)
  • Economic advancement: Vermont subsistence → Wisconsin independent farming → Minnesota established farming operation
  • Female occupational role: Martha Eliza documented as “Keeping House” (household management); unmarked but essential agricultural contribution

Economic Indicators and Advancement

Vermont Phase (1782–1850)

  • Status: Subsistence farming; modest property ownership or leasehold
  • Indicators: Multi-child household suggests family labor available
  • Advancement: 68-year tenure suggests economic stability, not prosperity

Wisconsin Phase (1850–1880)

  • Entry status: Advanced-age relocation (Wynat age 68); modest capital for migration
  • Settlement: Burlington Township (Racine) → Ripon Ward (Fond du Lac); shift suggests economic adaptation
  • Property: $50 value recorded (minimal); modest landholding
  • Advancement: Oliver established independent household and farm by 1860; economic independence achieved

Minnesota Phase (1880–1923)

  • Entry: Oliver Lewis age 57, established farmer; brought farming expertise
  • Settlement: Mower County agricultural region; established farming community
  • Household: 7-member farm family (1900) suggests productive agricultural operation
  • Advancement: Multi-child household, farm laborer sons engaged in operations suggests successful farm consolidation

Family Economic Narrative

Vermont to Wisconsin Transition (1850):

  • Patriarch Wynat age 68: significant relocation suggests economic push (opportunity) or pull (necessity)
  • Possible drivers: UK grain competition affecting American farmers, frontier land opportunity, family opportunity following earlier settlers
  • Wisconsin settlement: Lake Michigan/Erie Canal access enabled market participation

Wisconsin to Minnesota Transition (1880):

  • Oliver Lewis age 57: mature farmer relocating to new settlement region
  • Possible drivers: son generation (Oliver) seeking land expansion, wife (Elizabeth) family connections, Midwest agricultural consolidation
  • Minnesota settlement: Cedar River valley agricultural expansion, railroad access enabling market participation
  • Martha Eliza marries Arthur Prior (farmer): unified Lewis and Prior families in Mower County

Minnesota Consolidation (1880–1923):

  • Multi-generational consolidation: Oliver Lewis, Martha Eliza Prior, extended family
  • Extended household: sons engaged in farm labor; daughters in household
  • 43-year Minnesota residence (Martha Eliza 1880–1923): successful settlement, intergenerational commitment

Cross-Regional Comparisons

Vermont Farming (1782–1850)

  • Duration: 68-year primary residence
  • Occupational consistency: Farming throughout
  • Family size: Large (multi-child); labor-intensive agriculture
  • Outcome: Patriarch survived to age 68–80; migration to frontier at advanced age

Wisconsin Farming (1850–1880)

  • Duration: 30-year intermediate residence; transitional settlement
  • Occupational consistency: Farming throughout; farming across 3 Wisconsin counties (Racine, Fond du Lac, nearby areas)
  • Family changes: Patriarch (Wynat) transitioned authority to son (Oliver); generational succession visible
  • Economic advancement: Independent farming established for Oliver (son generation)
  • Urban interlude: Ripon Ward (1860) urban location; family adapted to non-rural setting while maintaining farming

Minnesota Farming (1880–1923)

  • Duration: 43-year consolidated settlement; permanent relocation
  • Occupational consistency: Farming sustained (Oliver until 1892, Martha Eliza household until 1923)
  • Family consolidation: Extended household (7+ members); multi-child farming operation
  • Economic outcome: Successful agricultural consolidation in premier corn belt region
  • Intergenerational: Multi-generational farm family established; inheritance patterns implicit

  • Occupational continuity: Farming as family identity across all regions; three-generation farming succession
  • Economic advancement: Vermont subsistence → Wisconsin independent farming → Minnesota consolidated farming shows economic trajectory
  • Generational analysis: Patriarch→son→granddaughter progression enables multi-generational study

For Economic Historians

  • Frontier settlement narratives: Vermont farmer relocation to Wisconsin/Minnesota frontier shows rural economic adaptation
  • Agricultural consolidation: 140-year farming progression shows successful land acquisition and intergenerational consolidation
  • Occupational resilience: Farming sustained despite regional transitions; agricultural occupation viable 1782–1923
  • Regional comparison: Vermont subsistence farming vs. Midwest frontier agricultural opportunity illustrates economic geography

For Migration Historians

  • Multi-stage migration: Vermont→Wisconsin (intermediate, 30 years) → Minnesota (final, 43 years) shows staged settlement pattern
  • Frontier expansion: Wisconsin/Minnesota settlement coincides with railroad expansion, agricultural development
  • Family chain migration: Extended household members, marriage alliances (Prior marriage) suggest network-driven settlement
  • Return migration: Martha Eliza brief South Dakota residence (1910) followed by Minnesota return shows settlement flexibility

For Social Historians

  • Family structure: Multi-generational household organization; patriarch→son→daughter progression shows family continuity
  • Gender roles: Martha Eliza “Keeping House” designation documents female household management role in farm family
  • Occupational identity: Farming as family identity across generations and regions; occupational inheritance pattern

Research Gaps and Limitations

Documentary Gaps

  • Pre-1850 Vermont residence: No census documentation; arrival in Vermont unknown; migration path unclear
  • Intermediate relocation (1840–1850): Possible Wisconsin pre-1850 settlement; 1840 census missing
  • Birthplace inconsistencies: Wynat Lewis recorded Vermont (1850 Racine) vs. Massachusetts (1860 Fond du Lac); explanation unknown
  • Extended family members: Darling, Marshall, Elizabeth identities and relationships unclear
  • Post-1900 continuation: No documentation post-1900 for Oliver Lewis; implied death c.1892 unconfirmed
  • South Dakota interlude (1910): Martha Eliza prior documented 1910 Brown County SD; relocation driver unknown

Occupational Documentation Gaps

  • Household production: Martha Eliza household management labor undocumented; agricultural contribution unmeasured
  • Farm operations: No documentation of farm acreage, livestock, crops, productivity
  • Economic value: No wage/income documentation; farm property value minimal ($50 recorded)
  • Labor relationships: Unclear whether farm laborers were family members or hired; employment relationships undocumented
