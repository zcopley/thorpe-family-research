# American Settlement and Migration Timeline

## Overview

The Thorpe family ancestors documented in this vault show a consistent pattern of Atlantic migration from UK (primarily 1754–1840) to American settlement in the Midwest (1840–1900). This page traces the geographic progression across census years and family branches.

## Migration Waves and Settlement Patterns

### Wave 1: Early Settlement (1830s–1850s) — Pennsylvania and Ohio

**Key Entry Points:**
- **Pennsylvania:** James Lemmon (b. 1779, arrived early 1800s) → documented in Ohio by 1850
- **Ohio:** Primary first-generation settlement location (27 individuals documented)
  - Jefferson County: [[People/Andrew Ault|Andrew Ault]] (1850 census, age 82)
  - Sandusky County: [[People/James Lemmon|James Lemmon]] household (1850)
  - Multiple townships: Lemmon, Blake, Risden, Ault families (1850–1880)

**Occupational Context:** Farmers establishing agricultural settlements; Ault, Lemmon families claim Ohio as birth region by mid-1800s

**Evidence:**
- 1850 US census: Lemmon household, Sandusky County, Ohio (Series M432, Roll 726)
- Pedigree timeline context: [[References/Shared Intake 2026-04-22 Pedigree Timeline Thorpe|Thorpe Pedigree Timeline]]

### Wave 2: Midwest Expansion (1850s–1870s) — Iowa, Wisconsin, Minnesota

**Key Settlement Corridors:**

#### Iowa (30 individuals documented)
- **Lemmon-Blake-Thorpe cluster:** Multiple counties documented
  - Linn County: [[People/George B Spicer|George B. Spicer]], Spicer-Risden families (1870s–1880s)
  - Blackhawk County: Spicer household (1880 census)
  - Earlier settlements: [[People/Charles Russell Spicer|Charles Russell Spicer]] (Iowa County, 1850s)
- **Settlement pattern:** Agricultural counties; farming primary occupation (1850–1900)
- **Generational depth:** 3–4 generations by 1880

#### Wisconsin (11 individuals documented)
- **Lemmon line settlement:** [[People/Wynat Lewis|Wynat Lewis]] (Wynant Williamson Lewis) documented Wisconsin 1850–1860
- **Early settlements:** Used as way-station before Iowa/Ohio final settlement
- **Occupations:** Farmers, laborers in agricultural townships

#### Minnesota (9 individuals documented)
- **Secondary settlement zone:** Later arrivals in 1870s–1880s
- **Families:** Palmer, Prior lines showed Minnesota settlement
- **Pattern:** Some families migrated Minnesota→Iowa→Ohio sequence

### Wave 3: Consolidation (1870s–1900) — Established Communities

**Stabilized Settlements:**

- **Ohio:** Sandusky, Jefferson, other rural counties — Ault, Lemmon, Tallman families established multi-generational farms
- **Iowa:** Linn, Blackhawk, Benton counties — Spicer-Risden primary settlement with continuous farming
- **Secondary sites:** Pennsylvania, Michigan, Minnesota — scattered individual placements

**Occupational Transition:** Farmer → Chair maker (Iowa manufacturing growth 1860s) → continued farming (occupational flexibility)

**Evidence:** 1870–1880 US censuses (T623–T627 series) showing settled households, multiple children born in state

## Migration Drivers and Context

### Economic Factors
- **UK rural decline (1840s):** Agricultural depression in Lincolnshire, Somerset (Bellamy, Sorrell lines)
- **US frontier opportunity:** Land availability in Ohio, Iowa, Wisconsin (1840–1870)
- **Industrial transition:** Manufacturing growth in Iowa (chair making 1860s) shows occupational adaptation

### Social/Family Factors
- **Chain migration:** Early settlers (Lemmon 1800s) → family network recruitment → broader settlement (1850s)
- **Marriage networks:** Spicer + Risden marriage → combined settlement in Iowa
- **Generational succession:** Multi-generational farms in Ohio, Iowa by 1880 suggest successful integration

### Transportation
- **Erie Canal era (1820s–1830s):** Early Pennsylvania/Ohio settlement via inland waterways
- **Midwest expansion (1850s):** Rail development enabled Iowa/Wisconsin settlement
- **Timing correlation:** Family settlement dates align with transportation infrastructure expansion

## Census Documentation of Migration

### 1850 Census Snapshot
- **Lemmon household:** Sandusky County, Ohio; ages suggest immigration 20–30 years prior (c.1820–1830)
- **Birthplace recorded:** "Pennsylvania" or "New York" (earlier migration stage)
- **Household composition:** Extended family, servants suggesting relative prosperity

### 1870–1880 Census Snapshot
- **Spicer-Risden:** Iowa multiple counties; children born Iowa, suggesting 15–20 years settlement
- **Occupational diversity:** Some farming, some skilled trades (moulder foundry, chair maker)
- **Birthplace patterns:** US-born children indicate second-generation settlement

## Research Implications

### Documented Patterns
- **Clear Midwest concentration:** Ohio (entry), Iowa (primary), Wisconsin (secondary), Minnesota (peripheral)
- **Occupational continuity:** Farming sustained across regions and generations
- **Generational progression:** 1st gen (migrant adults) → 2nd gen (US-born) → 3rd gen (established communities)

### Research Gaps
