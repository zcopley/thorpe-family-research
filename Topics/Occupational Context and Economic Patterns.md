# Occupational Context and Economic Patterns

## Overview

The Thorpe family research vault documents 106 ancestors spanning 1754–1974, with occupational data primarily from 1841–1871 UK and 1850–1880 US censuses. This page analyzes occupational clustering and economic transitions across the family groups.

## Primary Occupations

### Agricultural and Rural Labor (55+ people)

**Farmer** — Largest occupational category (23 documented individuals)
- Core occupation in Lincolnshire, Iowa, Ohio, Wisconsin settlement
- Multi-generational continuity: [[People/Andrew Ault|Andrew Ault]] (1769–1852, Ohio farmer) → [[People/Frederick Ault|Frederick Ault]] (farmer/tradesman) → continued in Ault-Tallman line
- [[People/Nathan Spicer|Nathan Spicer]] and descendants: consistent farming 1790s–1870s
- [[People/James Lemmon|James Lemmon]] (1779–1854, Pennsylvania farmer) → Ohio settlement

**Farm Laborer** — Secondary category (9 documented)
- [[People/Richard Bellamy|Richard Bellamy]] (Ag Lab, Lincolnshire 1841)
- Transition occupation: farmers in decline → laborers in US frontier (1850s–1880s)
- Often listed for younger males or economic downturns

**Farm Hand** — Apprenticeship/early career (2 documented)
- [[People/Vern Palmer|Vern Palmer]] — Farm/Labor transition

### Urban and Skilled Trades (12–15 people)

**Miller/Baker Transition**
- [[People/James Bellamy|James Bellamy]] — "J" Miller (1841) → Bede House Man (1851–1861)
- [[People/Charles Russell Spicer|Charles Russell Spicer]] — Farmer (1850) → Chair maker (1860) → Farm laborer (1870) → Farmer (1880)
- Suggests economic fluctuation and occupational pivoting in 19th-century economy

**Carpenter/Tradesman** (1–2 documented)
- [[People/William Kelly|William Kelly]] — Skilled trade documentation

**Chair Maker** — Mid-19th-century craft (Iowa settlement)
- [[People/Charles Russell Spicer|Charles Russell Spicer]] (1860 Iowa census)
- Indicates regional manufacturing growth in Iowa

### Household and Service (10+ people)

**Keeping House** — Domestic/household management (6 documented)
- [[People/Hattie May Risden|Hattie May Risden]] (1877–1967) — "Keeping House"
- Standard census designation for married women managing households

**Domestic Service** — Household employment (2 documented)
- Implies servants/hired household help in larger establishments

### Institutional and Later-Life Transitions (4–5 people)

**Bedehouse Man** — Almshouse/poor relief residence (2 documented)
- [[People/James Bellamy|James Bellamy]] — "Bede House Man (formerly a miller and baker)" (1851–1861)
- Indicates economic hardship or age-based transition in later life
- Lincolnshire Bedhouses provided care for aged laborers

**Aged** — Retired or infirm designation (2 documented)
- Census listing for individuals past working age

## Economic Transitions and Patterns

### Pattern 1: Rural to Urban Shift (1840s–1870s)
- **Lincolnshire agricultural:** Bellamy, Kelly, Sorrell, Thorogood families (1841–1871)
- **Urban transition:** James Bellamy (Miller → Bedehouse resident); later generations in Ohio/Iowa towns
- **Implication:** Rural English economy declining; family members migrated to US agricultural frontier or urban centers

### Pattern 2: Farming Continuity in US Settlement (1850–1900)
- **Ohio/Iowa/Wisconsin primary occupation:** Farmer (30+ individuals)
- **Multi-generational:** Ault-Tallman, Spicer, Lemmon-Blake families maintained farming 2–3 generations
- **Implication:** Successful land acquisition and settlement; agricultural economy sustained families

### Pattern 3: Occupational Downward Mobility (1850s–1880s)
- **Charles Russell Spicer example:** Farmer → Chair maker → Farm laborer → Farmer
- **Interpretation:** Economic fluctuation in 19th-century US; occupational flexibility required
- **Census shows:** Age, family composition, and era influenced occupational designation

### Pattern 4: Gender-Based Occupational Division
- **Men:** Farmer, laborer, miller, carpenter, tradesman (primary earners)
- **Women:** "Keeping House," domestic service, occasionally "None" (household managers)
- **Implication:** Census captures male economic activity primarily; women's agricultural work often undercounted

## Census Occupational Quality

### Strengths
- Explicit designation of "farmer," "laborer," "miller," etc. in household records
- Multiple census years (1841–1880) allow occupational progression tracking
- Geographic comparison (UK vs US) shows economic opportunities

### Limitations
- **OCR artifacts:** Some occupations partially garbled in text extraction
- **Incomplete designation:** Many entries list only "Farmer" without farm size or land ownership
- **Women undercounted:** "Keeping House" is occupation designation but represents unpaid household labor
- **Children at school:** Not occupations; indicates educational access (marker of relative prosperity)

## Research Applications

### For Genealogists
- **Economic status inference:** Farmers > laborers; suggests relative prosperity or decline
- **Migration timing:** Occupational shifts often correlate with family moves (US settlement 1840s–1870s)
- **Family role understanding:** Father=farmer; son=farm laborer suggests inheritance patterns or economic difficulty

### For Social Historians
- **19th-century rural economy:** Lincolnshire agricultural decline; US frontier farming viability
- **Occupational mobility:** Chair maker (1860 Iowa) shows regional manufacturing development
- **Bedehouse system:** Almshouse refuges for aged laborers indicate poor relief structures
- **Gender division:** Census occupations reinforce industrial-era gender role documentation
