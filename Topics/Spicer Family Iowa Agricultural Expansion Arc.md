# Spicer Family Iowa Agricultural Expansion Arc

## Overview

The Spicer family Iowa agricultural expansion from Iowa County (1850s) through Linn County consolidation (1880–1900) and beyond (1900–1974) represents an 80+ year multi-generational farming dynasty and successful frontier settlement narrative. Beginning with patriarch [[People/Nathan Spicer|Nathan Spicer]] (c.1796–1873) and progressing through son [[People/Charles Russell Spicer|Charles Russell Spicer]] (1822–1887), grandson [[People/George B Spicer|George B. Spicer]] (1864–1938), and great-grandson [[People/Lester Harold Spicer|Lester Harold Spicer]] (1906–1974), the family exemplifies sustained agricultural success, land consolidation through marriage (Risden family alliance), and multi-generational farming operation within a single Midwest agricultural region.

- **Family status:** Patriarch; established family farming tradition
- **Children:** [[People/Charles Russell Spicer|Charles Russell Spicer]] (1822–1887) documented as son; other children possible

**Early Farming Career (pre-1850):**
- Farming patriarch established by 1820s (Charles Russell b. 1822 indicates established household)
- Family farming origin in eastern states (Pennsylvania/New York likely)
- Relocation to Iowa frontier likely 1830s–1850s (timing inferred from son Charles Russell settlement in Iowa County 1850s)
- Patriarch role: established family farming identity and values; transmitted to sons

**Occupational Legacy:**
- Farming occupation became family identity; Nathan Spicer established agricultural expertise and values
