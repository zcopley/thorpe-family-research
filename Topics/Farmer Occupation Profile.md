# Farmer Occupation Profile

## Overview

The Farmer occupation represents the largest documented occupational category in the Thorpe family research vault, with 23 individuals documented across UK agricultural parishes (Lincolnshire) and US Midwest settlement areas (Ohio, Iowa, Wisconsin, Minnesota, Pennsylvania) spanning 1800–1900. Farming demonstrates sustained economic success, multi-generational continuity, and successful frontier settlement patterns in 19th-century America.

## Occupational Patterns and Economic Implications

### Pattern 1: Multi-Generational Farming Continuity

**Spicer Farming Dynasty (80-year span):**
- Nathan Spicer (patriarch c.1796) → Charles Russell (farmer, 1822–1887) → George B. (farmer, 1864–1938) → Lester Harold (farmer, 1906–1974)
- Four consecutive generations engaged in farming; clear inheritance of land and farming knowledge
- Demonstrates successful land acquisition, retention, and economic viability over 80+ years

**Lemmon-Tallman Farming Chain (70+ year span):**
- James Lemmon (farmer, 1779–1854, Pennsylvania → Ohio) → Mathew Lemmon (farmer, 1810s–?) → Uriah Blake Lemmon (farmer, 1808–1887) → Benjamin B. Tallman (farmer, 1841–1921)
- Ault-Tallman marriage alliance (Andrew Ault → Frederick Ault → Elizabeth Plomey Ault → Miller Mathias Tallman)
- Evidence of successful land consolidation through marriage

**Lewis Family Wisconsin-Minnesota Arc (60+ year span):**
- Oliver Warren Lewis (farmer, 1823–1892, Vermont → Wisconsin → Minnesota)
- Martha Eliza Lewis (Wisconsin-born, 1859–1923) → married Arthur Prior (farmer, 1853–?)
- Documented farming households in Racine County WI (1850), Fond du Lac County WI (1860), Mower County MN (1880–1900)

### Pattern 2: Household Size and Property Accumulation

**Large Farming Households (Indicators of Economic Success):**
- George B. Spicer household 1900: 8+ members (patriarch, wife, 5+ children); documented farm labor by multiple sons
- Bangle family households 1870: 8–9 members (patriarch, wife, multiple Wisconsin-born children); stable occupational designation
- Prior household 1900: 7 members (Arthur, Martha, 5 children); farm laborer sons indicate property sufficient for multi-family employment

**Property Wealth Indicators:**
- James Lemmon (1850): Household listed with property value indicators
- Lewis households: Multi-year presence in same location (Wisconsin 1850–1860, Minnesota 1880–1900) suggests land ownership
- Spicer household continuity: Multiple census documentation in same county suggests stable property holdings

### Pattern 3: Economic Resilience vs. Decline

**Successful Continuity (Majority Pattern):**
- Andrew Ault (age 82 in 1850, still farming): Economic independence maintained into advanced age
- James Lemmon (age 71 in 1850, still farming): 70+ year economic viability
- George B. Spicer (1880–1900, expanding household): Occupational stability, child-rearing in farming household
- Oliver Warren Lewis (Wisconsin/Minnesota farms, 1850–1880): 30+ year farming span across multiple states

**Occupational Vulnerability/Transition:**
- Charles Russell Spicer: Farmer (1850) → Chair maker (1860) → Farm laborer (1870) → Farmer (1880) — suggests economic fluctuation in 1860s–1870s
- James Bellamy: Miller/farmer (1841) → Bede House resident (1851+) — economic decline into institutional care (different trajectory from successful Midwest farmers)
- Richard Bellamy: Ag Lab (farm laborer) rather than farmer — suggests economic status below farmer-patriarch level

### Pattern 4: Generational Occupational Succession

**Sons Continuing Farming (Evidence of Inheritance):**
- Multiple families show clear "father farmer → son farmer" progression
- Some sons listed as "farm labor" or "working on farm" in early years, suggesting apprenticeship before independent farming
- Example: George B. Spicer age 16 as "farm laborer" (1880), later "farmer" (1900)

**Younger Generation Flexibility:**
- Farm labor designation more common for sons aged 10–25 than fathers
- May represent intermediate occupational stage before independent farming or economic downturns requiring non-farming work

## Geographic and Temporal Patterns

### UK Lincolnshire Agricultural Context (1841–1871)

**Farmer Prevalence:**
- Lincolnshire documented as grain-cultivation region; miller occupation (James Bellamy) aligns with grain processing
- Decline pattern (Bellamy to Bede House) reflects broader UK rural economic stress 1840s–1860s
- Most Lincolnshire family farmers migrated to US by 1850 rather than staying in declining UK agricultural region

### Ohio Frontier Settlement (1800–1900)

**Early Entry (1820s–1850s):**
- James Lemmon (1800s entry) and Andrew Ault (documented 1850, age 82 suggesting 1800s arrival) represent first-generation frontier farmers
- Lake Erie / Sandusky County location: accessible via Erie Canal (opened 1825), enabling agricultural product export
- Successful long-term settlement (50+ year spans documented) indicates viable agricultural frontier

**Consolidation (1850–1900):**
- Second and third generations (Spicer, Ault-Tallman, Lemmon families) maintained farming through 1880s–1900s
- Multi-family presence in same counties (Sandusky, Jefferson) by 1850 suggests network effects and chain migration

### Iowa Agricultural Expansion (1850–1900)

**Pioneer Period (1850s–1860s):**
- Charles Russell Spicer documented Iowa County 1850s; chair maker occupational transition (1860) suggests response to regional manufacturing growth
- Earlier settlement than Linn County—suggests gradual westward progression
- Occupational flexibility (farmer → chair maker → farmer) indicates economic response to local opportunities

**Primary Settlement (1880–1900):**
- George B. Spicer and Linn County emerge as primary settlement hub by 1880
- Cedar River valley agricultural excellence and rail access supported expansion
- Large multi-child households (Spicer 1900: 8+ members) indicate agricultural viability

### Wisconsin and Minnesota Secondary Settlement (1850–1900)

**Wisconsin Entry Point (1850–1860):**
- Racine and Fond du Lac County documented for Lewis family; described as intermediate stop before Minnesota settlement
- Less documentation than Ohio/Iowa, suggesting shorter-term residence or secondary settlement role
- Eau Claire County Bangle family (1860–1900) shows longer-term Wisconsin settlement pattern

**Minnesota Consolidation (1880–1900):**
- Mower County documented as primary Minnesota settlement (Lewis, Prior, Palmer families 1880–1900)
- Later settlement than Ohio (50 years later), suggests gradual Midwest geographic expansion
- Agricultural conditions similar to Iowa; documented farming households with multiple children

## Occupational Context: Why Farming Dominated

### Economic Viability in 19th-Century Midwest
- Land availability in US frontier areas (1840–1880) incentivized migration from UK
- Agricultural products (grain, livestock) sustained by rail and waterway transport (Erie Canal 1825, transcontinental rail 1860s–1880s)
- Family labor model: patriarch + sons engaged in farm work, wives managing household; efficient production

### Contrast with UK Decline
- UK rural economy declining (1840s–1870s) due to industrial competition and land consolidation
- Documented transition: Bellamy miller (1841) → decline (1851) indicates vulnerability of skilled UK rural occupations
- US frontier offered land ownership opportunity; UK rural laborers had no such path

### Family Continuity and Inheritance
- Farming enabled multi-generational property inheritance (unlike laborer or artisan occupations)
- Sons apprenticed to farming (farm laborer designation) with expectation of inheriting land
- Marriage alliances (Spicer + Risden, Ault + Tallman) consolidated land and expanded family farming operations

## Research Applications

### For Genealogists
- **Farmer status inference:** Indicates relative prosperity; farmers > laborers in economic status
- **Migration timing:** Farmer documentation 1850 (Ohio) → 1880 (Minnesota) shows generational westward expansion
- **Property ownership:** Farmers more likely to appear in land records; traceable through deeds and patents
- **Multi-generational identification:** Farming families more likely documented across multiple censuses

### For Economic Historians
- **Nineteenth-century rural economy:** Farmers sustained through 1800–1900; indicates successful agricultural settlement
- **Frontier expansion narrative:** Clear progression from Ohio (1800s) → Iowa (1850s) → Minnesota/Wisconsin (1880s)
- **Land settlement patterns:** Farming families demonstrate chain migration and network effects
- **Occupational continuity:** Despite industrial growth, farming remained dominant occupation through 1900

### For Social Historians
- **Family structure:** Large extended households (5–9 members) enabled farm labor; patriarch-centered economic model
- **Gender division:** Farmers (male), "keeping house" (female); occupational divide reflects industrial-era gender roles
- **Economic mobility:** Most farmers show stability or improvement; contrast with UK decline narratives
- **Generational succession:** Father-son farming chains demonstrate successful inheritance and economic planning

## Research Gaps and Limitations

### Gaps in Current Documentation
- **Land records not yet extracted:** Farm acreage, property value, ownership terms unknown for most farmers
- **Church records not incorporated:** Marriage of farmers, religious affiliation, community roles undocumented
- **Economic details:** Household property value, livestock, crop types, market connections not documented
- **Individual motivation:** Reasons for migration (economic necessity vs. opportunity seeking) inferred, not documented

### Census Limitations
- **Occupation designation variability:** "Farmer," "Ag Lab," "Farm labor," "working on farm" used inconsistently; unclear if exact economic roles
- **Women's labor undercounted:** "Keeping house" designation misses agricultural contributions (dairy, vegetable production)
- **Children listed as "at home":** Occupational role ambiguous; may indicate school attendance, household labor, or economic inactivity
- **Property value absent after 1860:** Cannot track wealth accumulation or decline in later censuses

## Next Steps for Research
