# The Essex Victuallers: From Coachman to Publican

The Thorogood branch of the family history project offers a unique glimpse into 19th-century English life, tracing a path from domestic service to the management of local public houses in the heart of Essex.

## The Rise of James Thorogood (1828–1880)

The story begins with **[[People/James Thorogood|James Thorogood]]**, who appears in the 1841 census as a 14-year-old male servant in the village of Felsted. Like many young men of his time, James started his career in the service of local landowners.

By 1851, at age 23, James had moved up to the role of **Coachman** for Thomas Chalk, a Commissioner of Land in Chelmsford. Ten years later, in 1861, he was still serving as a coachman at the Rectory in Little Waltham, working alongside **[[People/Mary Sorrell|Mary Sorrell]]**, who served as a housemaid. It was here, amidst the daily routines of the rectory, that the connection between the Thorogood and Sorrell families was forged.

## The Publican Life

The 1871 census reveals a significant transition. James Thorogood was no longer in domestic service; he had become a **Licensed Victualler** (a public house manager) on High Street in Wormley, Hertfordshire. 

Running a public house meant James was now a central figure in his community. His household in 1871 was busy and multi-generational, including his wife Mary, their son Frederick, and several lodgers who worked as laborers and ostlers. This period represents the height of the family's transition from service to independent business management.

## The Legacy of the Beehive and Whitehouse Farm

Through the **[[Topics/Great Baddow Oral History Extraction Index|Great Baddow Oral History]]**, we learn that the family's footprint extended to places like **Whitehouse Farm** and **Beehive Lane**. These names evoke the rural landscape of Essex where the Thorogoods and Sorrells lived and worked for generations before the family's later branches began the journey toward Iowa.

## Why This Story Matters

The Thorogood narrative provides a "vertical" view of social mobility in Victorian England. It shows how skills learned in service (as a coachman) could lead to the responsibility of running a public house, providing a stable foundation for the next generation—like **[[People/Frederick Thorogood|Frederick Thorogood]]**—who would eventually transition into the modern world of the railway and beyond.
