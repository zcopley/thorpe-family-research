# The Thorpe Ancestral Trail: From the Shenandoah to the Prairies

The ancestry of the Thorpe family follows a "Long Grey Trail" through some of the most historic landscapes in early America. While the Thorpe name was established in Iowa by the early 20th century, the roots of the family reach back to the **Shenandoah Valley** of Virginia and the river valleys of **Ohio**.

## The Virginia Origins: Harrison and Woodley

The trail begins in **Rockingham County, Virginia**, where families like the Harrisons and Woodleys lived along the "Long Grey Trail" (the historic road through the Shenandoah). **[[People/Nathaniel Harrison|Nathaniel Harrison]]** and **[[People/Mary Woodley|Mary Woodley]]** were part of the generation that lived through the American Revolution and the early years of the Republic. Their daughter, **[[People/Elizabeth Harrison|Elizabeth Harrison]]**, would eventually marry into the Tallman line, carrying the Virginia legacy westward.

## The Ohio Consolidation

The next stop on the trail was **Fairfield and Jefferson County, Ohio**. The Tallman family, led by patriarchs like **[[People/John Tallman|John Tallman]]** and later **[[People/Benjamin B Tallman|Benjamin B. Tallman]]**, established significant farming operations in the fertile Ohio soil. 

It was during this period that the family's service history continued to grow. **[[People/Miller Mathias Tallman|Miller Mathias Tallman]]** (son of Benjamin and [[People/Romancy Miller|Romancy Miller]]) was born in Ohio in 1841. His life would bridge the gap between the family's Ohio roots and their Iowa future.

## The Soldier of the Soldiers' Home

Miller Mathias Tallman's life is a story of transition. He began as a farmer in Ohio and Iowa, but his later years were defined by his service as a **Union Veteran**. Records show him and his wife, **[[People/Elizabeth Plomey Ault|Elizabeth Plomey Ault]]**, residing at the **Iowa Soldiers' Home** in Marshalltown for over a decade. This institution was a sanctuary for those who had served their country, and Miller's presence there highlights the patriotic thread that runs through the Thorpe ancestry.

## The Bridge to the Thorpes

The final link in the ancestral trail was forged when Miller's daughter, **[[People/Lenore Hetty Tallman|Lenore Hetty Tallman]]**, married **[[People/Uriah Blake Thorpe|Uriah Blake Thorpe]]**. This union brought together the Thorpe line with the deep Tallman/Harrison/Ault heritage. 

By the time the family settled in **Linn County, Iowa**, they carried with them over 150 years of American history—from the Shenandoah Valley to the Ohio frontier, and finally to the Iowa prairies.
