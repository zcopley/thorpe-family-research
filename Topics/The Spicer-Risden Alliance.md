# The Spicer-Risden Alliance: Farmers and Soldiers

The history of the Spicer branch in Iowa is defined by a powerful union between two different but complementary legacies: the industrious **Pioneer Farmer** and the decorated **Civil War Soldier**. This alliance was forged through the marriage of **[[People/George B Spicer|George B. Spicer]]** and **[[People/Hattie May Risden|Hattie May Risden]]**.

## The Farmer: George B. Spicer (1864–1938)

George B. Spicer was a "Linn County Consolidator." Born in 1864, he grew up in the family's emerging farming dynasty. By 1880, at age 16, he was already an apprentice in the family farming operation. George represented the second generation of Iowa-born Spicers who turned the raw prairie into a productive agricultural empire. His life was centered on the land, the seasons, and the steady expansion of the family's holdings in **Linn and Benton Counties**.

## The Soldier's Daughter: Hattie May Risden (1877–1967)

Hattie May Risden brought a different heritage to the family. She was the daughter of **[[People/Watson Moses Risden|Watson Moses Risden]]**, a hero of the Civil War and a prominent figure in the **Grand Army of the Republic (G.A.R.)**. 

Watson's story was one of intense service: he fought in the assaults on **Dalton, Rocky Face, and Buzzard's Roost**, and eventually rose to become the **Iowa Department Commander** of the G.A.R. Growing up in the shadow of her father's service, Hattie lived in a world where community leadership and veteran service were paramount.

## The Union of Two Fronts

When George and Hattie married, they brought these two worlds together. Their household in **Cedar Rapids** and the surrounding townships became a hub where the stability of the farming tradition met the prestige of the veteran community. 

This alliance is visible in the census records of the early 1900s, where we see the family flourishing with seven children, including **[[People/Lester Harold Spicer|Lester Harold Spicer]]**, who would carry the farming dynasty forward into the 1970s.

## Legacy of the Alliance

The Spicer-Risden union ensured that the family was not only rooted in the soil but also in the social and civic fabric of Iowa. The combination of agricultural expertise and community service defined the branch for over a century, providing the foundation for the family's lasting presence in the region.
