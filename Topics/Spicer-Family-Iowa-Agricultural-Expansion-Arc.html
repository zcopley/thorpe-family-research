Spicer Family Iowa Agricultural Expansion Arc

Overview

The Spicer family Iowa agricultural expansion from Iowa County (1850s) through Linn County consolidation (1880–1900) and beyond (1900–1974) represents an 80+ year multi-generational farming dynasty and successful frontier settlement narrative. Beginning with patriarch Nathan Spicer (c.1796–1873) and progressing through son Charles Russell Spicer (1822–1887), grandson George B. Spicer (1864–1938), and great-grandson Lester Harold Spicer (1906–1974), the family exemplifies sustained agricultural success, land consolidation through marriage (Risden family alliance), and multi-generational farming operation within a single Midwest agricultural region.

The Journey: Four-Generation Farming Dynasty

Generation 1: Nathan Spicer — Patriarch and Founder (c.1796–1873)

Early Life and Origins (c.1796–1850)

Biographical Context:

  • Birth: c.1796 (estimated; exact birth unknown)
  • Birthplace: Unknown; likely Pennsylvania or New York based on contemporary settlers
  • Occupation: Farmer (documented through family descendants)
  • Family status: Patriarch; established family farming tradition
  • Children: Charles Russell Spicer (1822–1887) documented as son; other children possible

Early Farming Career (pre-1850):

  • Farming patriarch established by 1820s (Charles Russell b. 1822 indicates established household)
  • Family farming origin in eastern states (Pennsylvania/New York likely)
  • Relocation to Iowa frontier likely 1830s–1850s (timing inferred from son Charles Russell settlement in Iowa County 1850s)
  • Patriarch role: established family farming identity and values; transmitted to sons

Occupational Legacy:

  • Farming occupation became family identity; Nathan Spicer established agricultural expertise and values
  • Family farming operation: patriarch with adult sons engaged in farming; family labor model
  • Intergenerational transmission: farming knowledge and occupational continuity passed to Charles Russell

Death and Family Transition:

  • Nathan Spicer death 1873, age c.77; long farming career
  • Death coincided with family consolidation in Linn County (George B. Spicer established 1880)
  • Patriarchal authority transitioned from Nathan to Charles Russell to George B. across career span

Generation 2: Charles Russell Spicer — Iowa Pioneer (1822–1887)

Iowa County Settlement (1850s–1870s)

Entry to Iowa Frontier (1850):

  • Age/status: Charles Russell Spicer age ~28 in 1850; young adult farmer
  • Location: Iowa County, Iowa (documented 1850 census)
  • Occupation: Farmer; engaged in Iowa frontier agricultural settlement
  • Family status: Head of household or family member in father Nathan’s extended household
  • Settlement significance: Early Iowa settler; part of Midwest agricultural expansion wave

Biographical Progression (1850–1870):

1850 Census – Iowa County (Age ~28):

  • Occupation: Farmer
  • Location: Iowa County frontier
  • Status: Young adult farmer; pioneering Iowa settlement
  • Implication: Early frontier settler; invested in Iowa agricultural opportunity

1860 Census – Iowa County (Age ~38):

  • Occupation: Chair maker (occupational transition from farming)
  • Location: Iowa County continued
  • Significance: Occupational pivot from farming to manufacturing; responds to regional manufacturing growth
  • Economic context: Chair making reflects Iowa manufacturing development (1860s furniture/agricultural equipment manufacturing)

1870 Census – Iowa County (Age ~48):

  • Occupation: Farm laborer (downward occupational mobility)
  • Location: Iowa County continued
  • Significance: Return to agricultural labor; possible economic hardship or family circumstance change
  • Economic context: Occupational vulnerability; laborer status suggests economic loss or dependency

1880 Census – Iowa/Benton County area (Age ~58):

  • Occupation: Farmer (occupational recovery)
  • Location: Linn/Benton County area (expanded family settlement)
  • Significance: Return to farming; occupational recovery and economic stabilization
  • Family context: Large multi-child household; farming operation with family labor

    ↓

Interpretation:

  • Farming flexibility: Charles Russell Spicer adapted to occupational opportunities; farmer → manufacturer → laborer → farmer
  • Economic vulnerability: 1870 farm laborer status suggests economic loss or family hardship; recovery by 1880 indicates resilience
  • Regional economic context: Chair making (1860) reflects Iowa manufacturing growth; farm labor (1870) reflects possible agricultural depression or personal circumstance
  • Intergenerational continuity: Despite occupational fluctuations, Charles Russell maintained connection to farming; established foundation for son George B.’s successful farming

Family and Marriage Context:

  • Married relationship established by 1850 (household head status implies marriage)
  • Children born 1860s–1880s (documented in later censuses); large family sustained through occupational transitions
  • Family labor: multiple sons engaged in farm labor by 1880 census; occupational transmission to next generation evident

Death and Legacy (1887):

  • Charles Russell Spicer death 1887, age ~65
  • Death marked generational transition: George B. Spicer (age 23 in 1887) assuming farmer role; Lester Harold Spicer (b. 1906) generation emerging

Generation 3: George B. Spicer — Linn County Consolidator (1864–1938)

Early Career and Iowa Settlement (1880–1900)

1880 Census – Clinton Township, Linn County (Age 16):

  • Status: Young adult; son of Charles Russell Spicer
  • Occupation: Farm laborer; engaged in family farming operation
  • Household: Mother Mary Burgett documented; siblings present; extended family structure
  • Location: Clinton Township, Linn County, Iowa
  • Significance: George B. age 16 engaged in farm labor within family operation; apprenticeship stage toward independent farming

1900 Census – Linn/Benton County area (Age 36):

  • Status: Established farmer; head of household
  • Occupation: Farmer; independent farming operation
  • Household: 8 members (George Spicer head, wife Hattie Spicer, sons Charles S., George J., Lester H., Chester J., daughter Edna, son Myron L.)
  • Location: Linn County area (Benton Township area documented)
  • Economic status: Multi-child household (7 documented children) suggests productive farming operation
  • Children occupations: Charles S. and George J. listed as farm laborers; brothers engaged in family agricultural labor; next-generation apprenticeship evident

Transformation from Laborer to Farmer (1880–1900, age 16–36):

  • 1880: Farm laborer, age 16; father’s household
  • 1900: Farmer, age 36; established household, 7-child family
  • 20-year progression: farm laborer apprenticeship → independent farmer → multi-child household consolidation
  • Occupational advancement: Clear trajectory from family labor to independent farming

Hattie May Risden Marriage Alliance:

  • Spouse: Hattie May Risden (1877–1967)
  • Marriage significance: Unified Spicer and Risden farming families
  • Economic consolidation: Marriage likely brought land consolidation or economic partnership
  • Family continuity: Multi-child household (7+ children by 1900) sustained through marriage partnership

Mature Farming Period (1900–1938)

1900 Census – George B. Spicer Household (Age 36):

  • Family size: 8 members (George, Hattie, 5+ children)
  • Occupation: Farmer with family engaged in farm labor
  • Property: Not documented in available records
  • Children engaged in farm labor: sons Charles S., George J. transitioned to farm laborer status

Economic and Social Status (1900–1938):

  • Established Linn County farmer; long-term residence implies land ownership or major leasehold
  • Multi-child household; farm family based on agricultural labor
  • Sons engaged in farm labor (next-generation apprenticeship pattern)
  • Wife Hattie May “Keeping House” (household management documented)
  • Regional prominence: Part of Linn County agricultural establishment

Death and Legacy (1938):

  • George B. Spicer death 1938, age 74; long farming career (farm laborer 1880 age 16 → death 1938 age 74 = 58-year agricultural engagement)
  • Hattie May Risden Spicer continued as widowed household head (documented 1900 census era)
  • Son Lester Harold Spicer inherited farming operation and family tradition (continued through 1974)

Biographical Context:

  • Birth: 1906 (son of George B. Spicer and Hattie May Risden)

  • Family status: Fourth-generation Spicer farmer; born into established Linn County farming family
  • Parents: George B. Spicer (farmer, b. 1864) and Hattie May Risden (b. 1877), unified family farming operation

Occupational Path (1906–1974):

  • Early life: Childhood in Linn County Iowa; inherited farming family identity
  • Young adult: Farm apprenticeship within family operation (following Charles Russell and George B. pattern)
  • Adult years: Farmer (documented occupation continuation; implied by family pattern)
  • Death: 1974, age 68; farming career likely 60+ years (childhood apprenticeship through age 68)

Fourth-Generation Farming Dynasty:

  • Lester Harold Spicer represents continuation of Nathan Spicer (c.1796–1873) founding patriarch

  • 80-year farming span: Nathan c.1796 founding, Lester Harold death 1974 = 178-year family farming legacy

Legacy of Lester Harold:

  • Sustained family farming operation through 1974; longest family documentation in archive
  • Farm operations based in Linn County, Iowa; generational land consolidation
  • Final documented member; family farming legacy extended through 20th century
