## Page Structure and Content

### Farm Laborer Occupation Profile
- **Population documented:** 9+ farm laborers across Lincolnshire UK (2), Ohio (3), Iowa (2), Minnesota (2+)
- **Occupational variations:** "Ag Lab," "Farm labor," "farm laborer," "working on farm" terminology inconsistency
- **Key patterns identified:**
  - Apprenticeship stage (young males age 10–25 in farm labor, progressing to farmer independence)
  - Downward mobility (Charles Russell Spicer example: Farmer 1850 → Chair maker 1860 → Farm laborer 1870 → Farmer 1880)
  - Occupational flexibility (farm laborer as labor market response to economic hardship or opportunity)
  - Father-to-son progression (documented examples: George B. Spicer, Oliver W. Prior, George W. Prior)
- **Economic context:**
  - Lower economic status than farmers; subsistence-level wages (inferred)
  - Occupational accessibility for displaced workers or young laborers
  - US frontier enabled upward mobility (farm laborer → farmer via land acquisition); UK offered no such pathway
- **Research gaps:** Wage documentation, employment relationship clarity (family vs. hired labor), post-census trajectories, child labor undercounting

### Household Management and Domestic Service Occupation Profile
- **Population documented:** 6+ women ("Keeping House"), 1–2 in domestic service
- **Documented individuals:**
  - Rebecca Blake (farmer's wife, undocumented but inferred household management)
  - Elizabeth Ault (farmer's wife age 83, no occupational designation despite lifetime household management)
  - Hattie May Risden (1877–1967, "Keeping House" designation across multiple censuses, 50+ year household management)
  - Martha Eliza Lewis (1859–1923, variable occupational designation: blank → "None" → "house keeping," showing census inconsistency)
- **Key patterns identified:**
  - Occupational invisibility (women's economic labor undercounted in census)
  - Gender-based occupational division (male wage labor vs. female unpaid household management)
  - Census inconsistency (blank fields, variable terminology, age-related changes)
  - Economic contribution unrecognized ("Keeping House" masks agricultural and household production)
- **Inferred productive contributions:**
  - Household production: dairy, eggs, vegetables, food preservation
  - Textile production: spinning, weaving, sewing, clothing manufacture
  - Food preparation: baking, cooking, food storage for large households
  - Child-rearing and education oversight
  - Household labor supervision (coordination with farm laborers or hired help)
- **Gender-based economic dependence:** Occupational status tied to marital status; no independent occupational pathway documented for married women
- **Research gaps:** Household labor documentation, wage/compensation records, occupational alternatives for unmarried women, economic value measurement of household production
